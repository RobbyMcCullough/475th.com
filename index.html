The History of 47 Fifth Street

A Victorian Home in the Early Days of Petaluma

Standing on the corner of Fifth and C Streets in Petaluma, California, the house at 47 Fifth Street is a surviving piece of the city’s early architectural history. Built around 1870, the home reflects the optimism and growth of Petaluma during the decades following the Gold Rush, when merchants, farmers, and tradespeople were transforming the small river town into one of Northern California’s most prosperous communities.

Petaluma in the 1870s

When the house was constructed, Petaluma was a thriving agricultural hub connected to the broader economy by the Petaluma River, allowing steamships to transport grain, dairy, and poultry products to San Francisco. By the late nineteenth century, the region would become internationally known as the “Egg Basket of the World.”

According to local historic records, 47 Fifth Street was built by Mr. Towne, the owner of Towne Drugstore, as a home for his bride in the early 1870s. Houses of this size and craftsmanship reflected a comfortable middle-class lifestyle in the growing town.

Architectural Style: Gothic Revival

The house is considered a Gothic Revival Victorian, a style popular in America between the 1840s and 1870s. Inspired by medieval European architecture, this home emphasizes strong vertical lines and ornate carpentry, featuring:

  • Steeply pitched roofs and cross gables
  • Decorative verge boards (gingerbread trim)
  • Fish-scale shingles and horizontal ship-lap siding
  • Ornamental wood detailing known as stick work
  • Tall, narrow vertical windows

The Craft of Victorian Carpentry

Victorian houses were built during a transitional period in American construction. Builders were beginning to use machine-milled lumber, but much of the craftsmanship remained highly manual. Most Victorian houses, including 47 Fifth Street, were built using balloon framing, a revolutionary construction technique that replaced heavy timber framing with lighter dimensional lumber running continuously from the foundation to the roofline.

The elaborate trim on Victorian homes was typically cut using scroll saws, often powered by foot treadles. These tools allowed carpenters to produce delicate wooden shapes that became known as gingerbread trim.

A Living Piece of History

More than 150 years after it was built, 47 Fifth Street remains a remarkable example of early Petaluma craftsmanship. Today the home stands as both a residence and a small architectural time capsule, preserving the artistry of the carpenters and builders who shaped Petaluma in the nineteenth century.
